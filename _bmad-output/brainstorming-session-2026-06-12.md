## Brainstorming Approach

**Selected Mode:** YOLO

**Session Goal:** Reframe `docs/game-design.md` from MVP-first to full-game-first, using the existing full-game promise from `docs/game-brief.md` and `docs/project-overview.md` as the product anchor.

**Techniques Available:**

- MDA Framework Exploration
- Player Fantasy Mining
- Core Loop Brainstorming
- Progression Curve Sculpting
- Relationship and Agency Moment Design
- Emotion Targeting
- Constraint-Based Creativity
- Failure State Design
- Reward Schedule Architecture
- Reference Blending

**Focus Areas:**

- Full three-year high school structure from Year 1 start to Year 3 graduation.
- Day, week, semester, year, and full-run gameplay loops.
- Relationship arcs as long-form memory-making rather than MVP teasers.
- Year-to-year emotional progression: entry, deepening, culmination.
- Locations as a scalable school-life network rather than a five-location MVP set.
- Academic, condition, money, travel, social media, and event systems as full-game systems.
- Endings shaped by academic direction, relationships, identity, routine, and future choice.
- MVP or vertical slice reframed as delivery phasing, not the product definition.

## Ideas Generated

### Idea: Beautiful Incompleteness As The Full-Run Thesis

**Source Technique:** Emotion Targeting / Player Fantasy Mining
**Description:** The full game should leave the player feeling that they could not have everything, and that this incompleteness made the run more personal rather than less successful.
**Potential:** This can become a core GDD thesis for three-year pacing, relationship density, event scheduling, endings, and replay value.
**Build-on ideas:** Tie the strongest emotional payoff to treasured memories, paths not taken, and the player's chosen circle rather than total completion.

### Idea: Found People Over Maximized Routes

**Source Technique:** Relationship and Agency Moment Design
**Description:** The relationship layer should prioritize the feeling of finding one's people: a believable circle of classmates who became important because the player chose them repeatedly over years.
**Potential:** This prevents the full game from becoming a checklist of all routes and supports relationship endings that feel social and personal.
**Build-on ideas:** Support close friendships, romance, loose friend groups, and missed connections as equally valid end-state textures.

### Idea: One Best Friend Plus Three-To-Five Known Arcs

**Source Technique:** Relationship and Agency Moment Design
**Description:** A strong full run should typically let the player form one best-friend-level bond and meaningfully experience roughly `3-5` classmate story arcs as good friendships or important connections.
**Potential:** This gives the full game a clear social capacity target without making every classmate route maximizable in one run.
**Build-on ideas:** Distinguish best-friend culmination, good-friend arc completion, romance commitment, and known-but-unfinished relationships in endings.

### Idea: Romance As Optional Strong Closeness

**Source Technique:** Player Agency Moments / Relationship Design
**Description:** Romance should be a strong and fully supported relationship form, but not the dominant promise of the game. Friendship, best-friend bonds, found family, and romance can all carry emotional value.
**Potential:** This keeps the game from becoming a dating sim while still supporting players who want romance as a central part of their run.
**Build-on ideas:** A romance partner may also be the best friend, but the game should also support best-friend and romance roles being separate.

### Idea: Romance Uses Social Capacity

**Source Technique:** Failure State Design / Reward Schedule Architecture
**Description:** Romance commitment should require time, attention, and emotional priority, reducing the player's practical capacity to deeply pursue other relationships in the same run.
**Potential:** This supports meaningful incompleteness and keeps romance from becoming a bonus layer added on top of maximal friendship completion.
**Build-on ideas:** Romance endings should reflect both commitment quality and what the player did or did not have room for elsewhere.

### Idea: Stable Core Cast With Rotating Periphery

**Source Technique:** Reward Schedule Architecture / Relationship Design
**Description:** The full game should keep a stable core classmate cast across all three years, while allowing peripheral characters to rotate in through classes, clubs, locations, events, work, and school-year changes.
**Potential:** This preserves long-term attachment while making Lakeview feel broader than a fixed route menu.
**Build-on ideas:** Core classmates carry full arcs; peripheral characters provide texture, hooks, short arcs, social context, and evidence that school life changes over time.

### Idea: Ten Core Classmates With Full Arcs

**Source Technique:** Constraint-Based Creativity / Relationship Design
**Description:** The full game should target `10` core classmates, each with a full multi-year story arc, while preserving rotating peripheral characters for world texture and shorter content.
**Potential:** This gives the school enough social breadth to support meaningful incompleteness while keeping the core cast finite and memorable.
**Build-on ideas:** A single run can meaningfully complete or strongly advance about `3-5` arcs, with one likely best-friend-level bond, so the full cast supports replay and paths not taken.

### Idea: Clubs As A Mid-Layer Identity System

**Source Technique:** Progression Curve Sculpting / Player Agency Moments
**Description:** Clubs and extracurriculars should be a major full-game mid-layer: players can choose `1-2` meaningful commitments that shape routine, friend groups, events, and identity across years.
**Potential:** Clubs give Year 1 and Year 2 stronger structure beyond lessons and hangouts, while supporting the fantasy of choosing who the student becomes.
**Build-on ideas:** Club arcs should exist, but remain lighter and less emotionally dense than core classmate story arcs.

### Idea: Club Arcs Are Smaller Than Classmate Arcs

**Source Technique:** Constraint-Based Creativity
**Description:** Each major club can have an arc, event chain, or seasonal milestone, but these arcs should support social context and identity rather than compete with full classmate routes.
**Potential:** This creates content variety without diluting the relationship promise or exploding narrative scope.
**Build-on ideas:** Club arc payoffs can unlock group memories, skill flavor, event participation, and ending texture rather than best-friend-level emotional climax.

### Idea: Four-To-Five Thematic Clubs

**Source Technique:** Constraint-Based Creativity
**Description:** The full game should target roughly `4-5` thematic clubs, such as science club or drama club, rather than a broad shallow activity roster.
**Potential:** This gives enough choice for identity expression while keeping each club distinct and content-supported.
**Build-on ideas:** Clubs should map to different social circles, activity rhythms, school events, skill flavors, and relationship opportunities.

### Idea: Remembered School Life

**Source Technique:** Emotion Targeting
**Description:** The final emotional read should be "these were my years," with a run remembered through routines, places, people, and choices rather than a scorecard.
**Potential:** This can guide outcome summaries, year transitions, memory albums, recurring location changes, and endgame reflection.
**Build-on ideas:** Use recurring seasonal moments and location memories to make three years feel accumulated rather than merely longer.

### Idea: Graduation Plus Memory Album Ending

**Source Technique:** Emotion Targeting / Moment Design
**Description:** The full game should culminate in a graduation sequence supported by academic/future outcomes, relationship epilogues, a memory-album recap, subtle reflection on unchosen paths, and an optional post-graduation glimpse.
**Potential:** This makes the ending feel like remembered school life rather than a purely numerical result screen.
**Build-on ideas:** The memory album can foreground chosen people, recurring places, club moments, academic turning points, and one or two visible absences without shaming the player.

### Idea: Subtle Paths-Not-Taken Reflection

**Source Technique:** Failure State Design / Emotion Targeting
**Description:** Endings should gently acknowledge meaningful incompleteness by hinting at missed or unchosen paths without turning them into failure states.
**Potential:** This reinforces the thesis that not doing everything is part of the game's emotional value.
**Build-on ideas:** Use yearbook notes, empty photo slots, distant acquaintances, or final social posts rather than explicit completion percentages.

### Idea: Discoverable Town-Scale Location Network

**Source Technique:** Core Loop Brainstorming / Environmental Storytelling
**Description:** The full game should model a substantial small-town location network rather than a permanently tiny venue set. Not every place is available at the start; locations become travel destinations after discovery through story, relationships, clubs, exploration, events, or invitations.
**Potential:** This keeps place-first discovery central across multiple years and makes Lakeview feel like a town the player gradually comes to know.
**Build-on ideas:** Use hubs and compact subspaces so the town can feel broad without requiring large navigation-heavy maps.

### Idea: Fifteen-To-Twenty-Five Travel Destinations

**Source Technique:** Constraint-Based Creativity / Environmental Storytelling
**Description:** The full game should target roughly `15-25` discoverable travel destinations across Lakeview, with compact level design and clear identities rather than large open-world traversal.
**Potential:** This supports the fantasy of gradually learning a town while keeping location content finite and purposeful.
**Build-on ideas:** Destinations can include major hubs, school spaces, club spaces, town venues, recovery spots, social spots, and event-linked places.

### Idea: Relationship-Driven Place Discovery

**Source Technique:** Player Agency Moments
**Description:** Some locations should be unlocked because a classmate, club, event, or story beat gives the player a reason and route to go there.
**Potential:** This ties exploration to memory-making and prevents the map from becoming a detached checklist.
**Build-on ideas:** A place can become meaningful because of who introduced it, not only because of what action it contains.

### Idea: Phone Apps As The Planning Interface

**Source Technique:** Core Loop Brainstorming / Reward Schedule Architecture
**Description:** The player's in-game phone should act as a compact planning interface, with social media as one app among others rather than the entire information layer.
**Potential:** This gives the UI an in-world frame for profiles, messages, calendar checks, map/travel planning, memories, and social discovery.
**Build-on ideas:** Phone apps can include calendar, map, social media, messages, class/grade info, and memory album features.

### Idea: Social Media As Planning, Memory, And Discovery

**Source Technique:** Player Agency Moments / Environmental Storytelling
**Description:** Social media should support known-classmate profiles, discovered preferences, availability, current known locations, memory/timeline records, social discovery, and light messaging.
**Potential:** This makes social planning readable without removing time, travel, money, and condition constraints.
**Build-on ideas:** Posts on classmate profiles can hint at preferences, moods, favorite activities, locations, and future invitation options.

### Idea: Lightweight Social Feed For Preference Reading

**Source Technique:** Reward Schedule Architecture
**Description:** Friends can publish profile posts that help the player infer what they like doing, where they spend time, or what they currently care about.
**Potential:** This makes preference discovery feel diegetic instead of purely menu-based.
**Build-on ideas:** Keep posting lightweight and authored enough to avoid becoming a heavy simulated social network.

### Idea: Two Strong Years And One Closing Year

**Source Technique:** Progression Curve Sculpting
**Description:** Year 1 and Year 2 should carry the strongest discovery, routine-building, relationship development, and school-life density. Year 3 should still be playable and meaningful, but its primary dramatic role is culmination, consequence, future choice, and closure.
**Potential:** This prevents the full game from treating all six semesters as equal content blocks and gives the three-year structure a readable emotional shape.
**Build-on ideas:** Year 1 asks "Where do I fit?" Year 2 asks "Who am I choosing to become close to?" Year 3 asks "What do I carry forward?"

### Idea: Academics As A Co-Equal Ending Driver

**Source Technique:** Progression Curve Sculpting / Failure State Design
**Description:** In the full game, academics should be nearly as important as relationships for long-term outcomes. Grades, subject direction, study patterns, and school performance shape future paths rather than serving only as pressure or failure prevention.
**Potential:** This gives Year 3 meaningful future-choice stakes and makes school life structurally central across all three years.
**Build-on ideas:** Academic direction can combine with clubs and relationships to form endings such as science-oriented, arts-oriented, socially grounded, uncertain, high-achievement, recovered-from-struggle, or relationship-prioritized futures.

### Idea: Academic Failure Ends The Run With Checkpoint Recovery

**Source Technique:** Failure State Design
**Description:** Academic semester failure should be a hard run-ending state, but the game should maintain automatic recovery saves from roughly `3` days, `2` weeks, and `2` months earlier and offer them when failure occurs.
**Potential:** This preserves academic stakes while protecting players from losing an entire long-form run without practical recovery options.
**Build-on ideas:** Failure presentation should explain causes clearly and frame checkpoint loading as recovery from a known academic collapse rather than hidden punishment.

### Idea: Year 1 Semester 1 Vertical Slice As Delivery Phase

**Source Technique:** Constraint-Based Creativity / Prototype Planning
**Description:** The former MVP should be reframed as the Year 1 Semester 1 vertical slice and first delivery phase, not as the defining product shape of the GDD.
**Potential:** This aligns `docs/game-design.md` with the full-game promise while preserving practical implementation sequencing.
**Build-on ideas:** The vertical slice should prove the daily loop, school pressure, after-school freedom, social discovery, first relationship content, phone planning, compact locations, exam resolution, and academic failure floor.

## Themes and Patterns

- The full-game emotional thesis is remembered school life shaped by beautiful incompleteness and found people.
- The social layer targets `10` core classmates, while one run supports roughly `3-5` meaningful arcs and usually one best-friend-level bond.
- Relationships and academics are nearly co-equal long-term pillars, both shaping Year 3 and graduation outcomes.
- Year 1 and Year 2 carry the strongest discovery, growth, relationship development, and routine-building; Year 3 focuses on closure and future choice.
- Clubs provide a mid-layer identity system with `4-5` thematic options and lighter arcs than core classmate stories.
- Lakeview should become a discoverable town-scale network of roughly `15-25` travel destinations, unlocked through story, relationships, clubs, events, and exploration.
- The in-game phone frames planning through apps such as calendar, map, social media, messages, class/grade info, and memory album.
- Social media supports planning, memory, discovery, messaging, and preference reading through profiles and lightweight posts.
- Academic failure remains a hard run-ending state, but checkpoint recovery protects long-form play from catastrophic hidden loss.
- The former MVP should be renamed and reframed as the Year 1 Semester 1 vertical slice and first delivery phase.

## Promising Combinations

- **Relationship discovery plus location discovery:** Classmates and clubs introduce places, making the map a record of social memory.
- **Academics plus clubs plus future paths:** Subject strengths and extracurricular commitments infer future direction without formal track selection.
- **Memory album plus paths not taken:** Graduation recap can celebrate chosen people and places while subtly acknowledging unchosen possibilities.
- **Best friend plus romance capacity:** Romance can overlap with or differ from best-friend status, but both consume limited social attention.
- **Stable cast plus rotating periphery:** Long-term attachment remains focused while the school and town still feel populated and changing.

## Session Summary

### Most Promising Concepts

**Top Pick: Remembered School Life Through Beautiful Incompleteness And Found People**

The full game should end with the player feeling that these were their years: personally chosen, emotionally incomplete, and defined by the people and places they invested in. This concept is the strongest GDD anchor because it turns incompleteness from a content limitation into a product thesis.

**Runner-up: Relationships And Academics As Co-Equal Long-Term Pillars**

The full game should treat social life and academic direction as nearly equal drivers of long-term outcomes. Classmate arcs, best-friend bonds, romance, grades, subject strengths, clubs, and future paths should all shape the graduation result.

**Honorable Mention: Discoverable Lakeview With Phone-Driven Planning**

Lakeview should be a discoverable town-scale location network supported by in-game phone apps. Social media, messages, map, calendar, class/grade info, and memory features make planning diegetic while tying location discovery to relationships and story.

### Key Insights

- The full game should target three school years from Year 1 entry to Year 3 graduation.

- Lakeview should target roughly `15-25` discoverable travel destinations, with places unlocked through story, relationships, clubs, events, and exploration.
