## Running Idea Capture

### Idea: Filmowe Liceum Przezyte Swiadomie

**Source Technique:** Emotion Targeting, Player Fantasy Mining
**Description:** The full-run emotional residue should combine finding people the player did not know they needed, consciously reliving a high-school-like experience, and maturing without fixing everything. The fantasy is not time travel or perfection, but a second chance to inhabit the emotional shape of high school films with more intention.
**Potential:** Strong north star for the NDD because it links the high-school-film fantasy with the GDD thesis of beautiful incompleteness and found people.
**Build-on ideas:** Graduation should feel like the end of a personal coming-of-age film; memory album entries should preserve both chosen friendships and unfinished almost-moments.

### Idea: Pelne Licealne Doswiadczenie Ponad Gatunkiem

**Source Technique:** Ludonarrative Harmony, Emotion Targeting
**Description:** The narrative should not anchor too narrowly on "high school film" as a genre imitation. Instead, it should synthesize the broader high-school experience: adolescent problems, school rituals, social identity, clubs, exams, town hangouts, pressure, belonging, missed chances, and graduation.
**Potential:** Keeps the NDD broad enough to support multiple tones and classmate arcs without making the game feel like a parody or genre checklist.
**Build-on ideas:** Treat familiar high-school motifs as ingredients, not the promise; every motif should be grounded in a teenager's concrete problem or desire.

### Idea: Osiem Zrodel Nastolatkowej Narracji

**Source Technique:** Emotion Targeting, Player Agency Moments
**Description:** The core narrative problem set should include identity, belonging, future pressure, first closeness, shame and reputation, ambition and rivalry, light-touch mental strain, and off-screen family pressure. These themes should feed classmate arcs, school events, phone content, academic stakes, and graduation outcomes.
**Potential:** Strong thematic palette for the NDD and cast design; broad enough for 10 classmates while staying grounded in adolescent experience.
**Build-on ideas:** Each major classmate arc can own one primary teenage problem and one secondary tension; player choices can either support, avoid, escalate, or misunderstand those tensions.

### Idea: Uwazny Katalizator

**Source Technique:** Player Agency Moments, Ludonarrative Harmony
**Description:** The player should mostly function as witness, catalyst, and safe person in classmates' stories. They notice hidden tensions, create space for honesty, and help others move toward decisions, but they do not solve classmates' lives or become the universal hero of every arc.
**Potential:** Aligns with relationship rewards as memory-making rather than power fantasy; prevents classmate arcs from becoming savior narratives.
**Build-on ideas:** Arc milestones should often be "they trusted me with this" or "my presence changed the moment" rather than "I fixed them".

### Idea: Uczen Ze Wsi Wyslany Do Lakeview

**Source Technique:** Player Fantasy Mining, Environmental Storytelling
**Description:** The player character comes from a small village without a high school. When they reach high-school age, their parents send them to a dormitory in Lakeview, the nearest town with a high school, despite it being several hours away by bus. The story begins as a practical family decision and a necessary move, not as a melodramatic secret.
**Potential:** Excellent narrative justification for the dormitory, outsider perspective, discovery of Lakeview, homesickness, family pressure, money constraints, and the Second-Chance Student Life fantasy.
**Build-on ideas:** The opening bus arrival can carry both anticipation and displacement; phone calls/messages from home can contrast village expectations with Lakeview identity formation.

### Idea: Year 1 Pytanie Czy Dam Rade

**Source Technique:** Emotion Targeting, Progression Curve Sculpting
**Description:** The protagonist's Year 1 tension should combine parental pressure, excitement about freedom, and anxiety about self-reliance. They wonder what school will be like, what people they will meet, whether they can handle dormitory life, and whether they can justify the family's decision to send them away.
**Potential:** Gives Year 1 a grounded emotional engine without making the protagonist overly tragic; supports cozy pressure and readable player agency.
**Build-on ideas:** Early scenes can contrast practical tasks with emotional stakes: unpacking, first schedule check, first school walk, first message home, first after-school choice alone.

### Idea: Wieczorny Przyjazd Do Dormitory

**Source Technique:** Moment Design, Environmental Storytelling
**Description:** The intro begins with the protagonist arriving in Lakeview in the evening. They must find the way to the dormitory, check in, reach their room, orient themselves through the phone, and go to sleep. The next day is the first day of the school year.
**Potential:** Strong first memory seed and practical tutorial for location, phone, dormitory, solitude, and the player's new life rhythm.
**Build-on ideas:** The first view of Lakeview should feel ordinary to locals but large to the protagonist; the dormitory room should be quiet enough to let anxiety and anticipation land before sleep.

### Idea: Pierwsze Kontakty Sa Praktyczne

**Source Technique:** Environmental Storytelling, Verbs Before Nouns
**Description:** The intro's first social interactions should be practical rather than dramatic: the protagonist asks passersby for directions to the dormitory, then checks in with the dormitory receptionist. These contacts teach that Lakeview is inhabited and readable without immediately foregrounding the core cast.
**Potential:** Keeps the opening grounded and lets the first school day carry the stronger classmate reveal energy.
**Build-on ideas:** Passersby can provide small tonal variation; the receptionist can introduce dorm rules, curfew expectations, and a gentle hint that many students feel lost on the first night.

### Idea: Core Cast Jako Stereotypy Z Prawda Pod Spodem

**Source Technique:** Constraint-Based Creativity, Ludonarrative Harmony

**Potential:** Gives immediate readability while preserving depth; players can quickly understand the school social map, then discover the person under the label.
**Build-on ideas:** Each classmate's social media profile can initially reinforce the stereotype, while route scenes and discovered preferences reveal contradictions.

### Idea: Goth Alt Kid Deep Arc Accepted

**Source Technique:** Character Deep Dive, Emotion Targeting
**Description:** The accepted deeper Goth / Alt Kid arc centers on tenderness as a risk of losing control. She wants to be seen, but fears that if someone sees her truly, the relationship will never return to normal. She writes vulnerable messages at night, then deletes or retreats from them in the morning.

**Three-Year Shape:**

- Year 1 Seed: A sincere, strange, witty, overexposed night message arrives at 02:17. In the morning she deletes it or says, "ignore that. sleep is a hostile editor."
- Year 2 Escalation: A fragment of a private message, note, or text circulates through school as microviolence: screenshots, half-jokes, locker comments. Others misread it as romantic drama, while the real fear is that friendship can leave too.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, she lets the player read something before deleting it and does not retreat the next morning. If unfinished, the final draft remains unsent.

**Memory Motif:** Phone under the pillow, a draft message at 02:17, blinking cursor after the final sentence.

**Player Agency Rules:** The player should not save or fix her. Meaningful choices include not pressing after a night message, interrupting microviolence without heroic spectacle, asking what she needs now rather than demanding an explanation, respecting her humor as part of the truth, and treating her normally the next day after vulnerability.

**Voice Lines:**

```text
"Relax. If I were being dramatic, there would be better lighting."

"I deleted it because morning me is a coward with excellent grammar."

"Don’t look so honored. I let you read one sentence, not my autopsy."

"People keep thinking the sad part is wanting someone. It’s not. It’s wanting someone to stay normal after they know."

"I’m fine. And before you say that sounds fake, congratulations, you have ears."
```

**Potential:** This is a strong signature arc because it is intimate, phone-native, dormitory-compatible, friendship- and romance-compatible, and highly aligned with the player's role as safe person rather than savior.
**Build-on ideas:** Use school lockers, dormitory laundry room, Lakeview water bench, library, open mic, and night phone scenes as recurring environments.

## Full Cast Bible Draft

This cast bible is full-game-first. It defines the target ten-classmate social layer across all three school years. Any Year 1 Semester 1 delivery slice should reveal early seeds from this full structure rather than redefining the cast around MVP needs.

### 1. Fashion Queen Bee

**School Mask:** Social center, fashion authority, polished queen bee whose style and approval shape the hallway weather.

**Hidden Drama:** Her mother pressures her toward modeling, beauty, social superiority, and public image. Under that pressure she once rejected a beloved friend and still regrets it. A clothing item or accessory from that friend keeps the betrayal physically present.

**Core Want:** To be admired without having to be curated.

**Core Fear:** That if she stops performing beauty and superiority, she will be ordinary, replaceable, or unloved.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can become a witness to the person beneath the image and a catalyst for small acts of honesty, but cannot force reconciliation or absolve her guilt.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: The player first sees her social precision and style control. A too-careful reaction to an old accessory reveals that one item matters more than the outfit.
- Year 2 Escalation: Modeling pressure increases through a contest, school campaign, or family-arranged opportunity. The betrayed friend returns to her social orbit, making image and loyalty collide.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, she publicly acknowledges the truth of the old friendship and stops treating beauty as proof of worth. If unfinished, she remains iconic but emotionally distant in the memory album.

**Memory Motif:** A scarf, hair clip, necklace, or dress detail from the friend she betrayed.

**Possible Outcomes:** Distant icon, unfinished regret, repaired friendship witness, close friend, romance if route tone supports mutual vulnerability without making the player her absolution.

### 2. Athlete

**School Mask:** Confident team figure, physically capable, competitive, associated with school pride and visible wins.

**Hidden Drama:** A wrong-witness story sits under the surface. They saw or misread an event, stayed silent or spoke with false certainty, and someone else was hurt by that version. They also carry forbidden tenderness: softness they do not know how to show safely.

**Core Want:** To be strong without being trapped by strength.

**Core Fear:** That admitting uncertainty will expose them as cowardly or disloyal.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can stand beside them as they re-examine what they saw and what they owe, but cannot decide the truth for them.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: They appear sure of themselves in competition. A quiet post-game moment near the gym hints at a specific person or place they avoid.
- Year 2 Escalation: The old event resurfaces. Their reputation, team loyalty, and self-image clash with the possibility that their certainty caused harm.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, they choose truth over clean team reputation. If unfinished, they may still win publicly while carrying the private cost of what was never corrected.

**Memory Motif:** An empty bench by the field or gym after practice.

**Possible Outcomes:** Respectful distance, unresolved guilt, trusted witness, close friend, romance grounded in being allowed to be gentle.

### 3. Nerd / Science Geek

**School Mask:** Hyper-prepared academic and science kid: protocols, schedules, backup pens, notes about notes, and an almost comic need for order.

**Hidden Drama:** They write letters to a future self who will finally have friends. They are not simply a natural genius; they compulsively translate chaos into plans because social spontaneity terrifies them. They also keep a small archive of abandoned objects as evidence of other people's unnoticed lives.

**Core Want:** To be understood without having to become socially fluent first.

**Core Fear:** That friendship is a variable they can never solve.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can be the first person who treats their systems as meaningful without letting those systems replace real connection.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: The player discovers a letter to a future self with friends and a labeled box of found objects.
- Year 2 Escalation: A science project or competition demands collaboration that cannot be reduced to a protocol. Their attempts to control uncertainty start hurting others.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, they create a class time capsule that leaves room for people rather than cataloging them. If unfinished, they succeed academically but their final letter still reads like survival instructions.

**Memory Motif:** A found-object box with handwritten labels.

**Possible Outcomes:** Known eccentric, unfinished almost-friend, close friend, best friend, romance built around patient mutual translation.

### 4. Art Kid

**School Mask:** Visual artist, sketchbook carrier, mural dreamer, zine maker, someone always half-drawing another world in the margins.

**Hidden Drama:** Their family expects a practical trade or career and sees art as a dead-end. To prove art can earn money, they secretly make commercial work under someone else's name or style, but every success feels like betrayal of their own voice.

**Core Want:** To make something unmistakably theirs and have it count.

**Core Fear:** That their real art is beautiful but useless.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can witness the difference between work that sells and work that tells the truth, but cannot choose their future for them.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: The player sees sketches everywhere, then discovers a commercial graphic signed under another name or made in an borrowed style.
- Year 2 Escalation: Family pressure intensifies through work, apprenticeship, or a practical career track. Paid art and personal art pull further apart.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, they present an imperfect personal work under their own name. If unfinished, they gain commercial success while hiding the sketchbook that mattered.

**Memory Motif:** An unfinished mural or sketchbook with torn-out pages.

**Possible Outcomes:** Distant admirer, unfinished collaborator, close friend, romance through shared creative risk.

### 5. Skater Rebel

**School Mask:** Boundary-testing skater, shortcut expert, roof-and-back-alley navigator, visibly allergic to rules.

**Hidden Drama:** Home is not emotionally safe enough, and skating gives them a reason not to return too early. Beneath the rebel mask is a sharp sensitivity to other people's pain. They know informal safe places around Lakeview because they have needed them.

**Core Want:** Freedom that is not just escape.

**Core Fear:** That staying anywhere long enough means being trapped.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can become someone they show safe places to, not someone who fixes home or turns them respectable.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: They know every shortcut and forbidden-feeling place. A minor injury reveals they would rather stay out than call home.
- Year 2 Escalation: The school, town, or family threatens the places they use to breathe. Their hidden protectiveness surfaces when younger students need refuge.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, they help shape a legal safe gathering place without pretending home is healed. If unfinished, they keep vanishing and leave only short messages saying not to worry.

**Memory Motif:** A safe-place map drawn on skateboard grip tape.

**Possible Outcomes:** Unreachable rumor, unfinished confidant, close friend, best friend, romance if freedom and staying can coexist.

### 6. Goth / Alt Kid

**School Mask:** Black clothes, sharp replies, alternative elegance, controlled distance, and the ability to make a room feel less honest by noticing everything.

**Hidden Drama:** She is deeply tender but terrified that visible vulnerability will change how people treat her. She sends sincere messages late at night, then deletes or retreats from them in the morning.

**Core Want:** To be seen and still treated normally afterward.

**Core Fear:** That naming what matters gives someone a weapon or makes the relationship unrecoverable.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can be a safe person by not pressing, not dramatizing, and staying normal after glimpses of vulnerability.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: At 02:17, she sends a sincere, strange, overexposed message. In the morning she deletes it or says, "ignore that. sleep is a hostile editor."
- Year 2 Escalation: A private message, note, or text fragment circulates through school as microviolence: screenshots, half-jokes, and locker comments. Others misread it as romantic drama when it is really about fear that friendship can leave too.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, she lets the player read something before deleting it and does not retreat the next morning. If unfinished, the final draft remains unsent.

**Memory Motif:** A phone under the pillow, a draft at 02:17, and a blinking cursor after the final sentence.

**Possible Outcomes:** Distant mystique, unfinished unsent message, close friend, best friend, slow-burn romance based on trust with language rather than rescue.

### 7. Student Council Organizer

**School Mask:** Clipboard, schedules, events, keys, volunteer rosters, the person who makes school life function.

**Hidden Drama:** Their kindness works like a contract. They help because they believe belonging must be earned. Organizing lets them enter every room without facing whether anyone would invite them as a guest.

**Core Want:** To be chosen when they are not useful.

**Core Fear:** That if they stop helping, they disappear from everyone else's life.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can notice the person after the event ends and can help them experience presence without productivity.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: They are always helpful and always needed. The crack appears when they clean up alone after everyone else leaves.
- Year 2 Escalation: A major school event goes wrong and the kindness contract breaks. They must confront whether help offered as currency is still kindness.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, they hand the clipboard to someone else and stay because they want to. If unfinished, their name appears in every thank-you list but few memories.

**Memory Motif:** A sticker-covered clipboard from three years of events.

**Possible Outcomes:** Helpful acquaintance, unfinished dependency, close friend, best friend, romance with someone who sees them off-duty.

### 8. Teacher's Pet

**School Mask:** Perfect answers, clean notebooks, teacher approval, institutional favorite, always correct and prepared.

**Hidden Drama:** Good grades are alibis. At home, mistakes are treated like interrogations, so every grade, signature, and teacher praise becomes evidence of innocence. Friendship can feel dangerous when closeness might become information to explain or defend.

**Core Want:** To make a mistake and still be safe.

**Core Fear:** That one visible flaw will become an accusation.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can help normalize imperfection by staying calm around small failures, but cannot rewrite the home environment.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: They seem exemplary until a minor error triggers disproportionate panic.
- Year 2 Escalation: A school conflict requires admitting uncertainty or defending someone without complete proof. Intimacy feels like a cross-examination.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, they allow one visible flaw to remain and do not turn it into a trial. If unfinished, they graduate with honors but speak like every sentence has been prepared for judgment.

**Memory Motif:** A red pen or corrected page that was not torn out.

**Possible Outcomes:** Polite distance, unfinished performance, close friend, romance if safety around imperfection becomes mutual.

### 9. Activist

**School Mask:** Petitions, posters, justice language, public questions, moral intensity, unwillingness to let unfairness pass quietly.

**Hidden Drama:** Their father sits on the city council and is tied to decisions the student opposes. The family story of public service contains a lie or compromise, but the father is not a flat villain.

**Core Want:** To build a moral voice that is not merely an argument with their father.

**Core Fear:** That they are inheriting the same compromises they condemn.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can witness the difference between righteous performance and difficult truth, but cannot settle the family conflict.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: The player sees petitions and sharp public questions. A city project with the father's name creates a reaction more personal than political.
- Year 2 Escalation: The inherited lie becomes more complicated. The father may have made harmful compromises, but not without context or consequence.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, they lead a campaign or speech that is neither copied from the father nor defined only against him. If unfinished, they win a small public issue but remain trapped in his shadow.

**Memory Motif:** A protest poster with corrections written on the back of a family invitation.

**Possible Outcomes:** Distant firebrand, unresolved ideological ally, close friend, romance through shared courage and disagreement.

### 10. Working Poor Student

**School Mask:** Busy, practical, older than their years, often absent after school, careful with money, hard to schedule.

**Hidden Drama:** A sick mother, a little sister, a completely absent father, and after-school work have made them the household's second adult. Their adolescence is full of empty places where ordinary teen life should have been.

**Core Want:** To be allowed one thing that belongs only to them.

**Core Fear:** That rest or joy will make the family collapse.

**Player Relationship Role:** The player can make individual moments easier and less lonely, but cannot rescue the family or solve poverty.

**Three-Year Arc:**

- Year 1 Seed: They disappear after school, count money, answer calls from their sister, and ask for help only indirectly.
- Year 2 Escalation: Work, caregiving, grades, and relationships collide. The player can help protect moments, not remove the burden.
- Year 3 Payoff: If advanced, they allow themselves one thing just for them: a class photo, application, trip, or evening without the phone. If unfinished, they finish functionally, but the memory album shows absences from events they could not attend.

**Memory Motif:** A receipt, bus ticket, or shopping list with homework written in the margin.

**Possible Outcomes:** Distant responsibility, unfinished absence, close friend, best friend, romance if care does not become another obligation.

## Relationship Ending Rules Draft

**Accepted Principle:** Relationship endings should not be a large standalone taxonomy of labels such as unfinished, repaired, distant, or unresolved. They should be authored interpretations of simple system state: relationship stage, arc progress, arc completion, best friend, romance, and whether the player was present for the Year 3 culmination. Conflict should be represented inside arc progress rather than as a separate system state.

These rules are full-game-first and should not introduce a large outcome enum. Relationship endings should be generated from simple system state plus authored narrative interpretation.

### System State Inputs

Relationship epilogues and memory album entries should read from:

- **Relationship Stage:** stranger, acquaintance, comfortable, close, very close.
- **Arc Progress:** which key story beats were seen across Year 1, Year 2, and Year 3.
- **Arc Completion:** incomplete or complete, with route-specific interpretation.
- **Special Bond:** none, best friend, romance. Best friend and romance may overlap but do not have to.
- **Final-Year Presence:** whether the player was present for the Year 3 culmination or last-chance scene.

### Narrative Interpretation Rules

- Do not expose many player-facing ending labels for partial relationships. Let the memory album and epilogue prose carry nuance.
- Incomplete relationship endings should use a classmate-specific tone based on that character's arc, with warm incompleteness and light regret without punishment as the default filter.
- A high relationship stage without arc completion should feel warm but unfinished, not like a failed checklist.
- Arc completion does not always mean the player became the classmate's most important person.
- A low relationship stage with a few important beats can still create a meaningful remembered almost-connection.
- Conflict repair should change the tone of the ending, not merely restore a numeric relationship value.
- Missed Year 3 culmination should not erase prior closeness, but it should leave a visible sense of distance, timing, or paths not taken.
- Best friend status should create a stronger graduation presence and memory album emphasis than ordinary close friendship.
- Romance should be a distinct commitment path, not a superior version of friendship.
- Romance plus best friend should be possible, but romance without best-friend status should also have a valid tone.
- A completed friendship arc and a completed romance arc may differ in intimacy, future promise, and memory language, but both should feel complete.
- Best friend and romance are separate forms of closeness. Best friend represents the most important everyday presence in the player's school years. Romance represents chosen romantic intimacy and commitment. They can overlap or remain separate.
- No-romance runs should remain narratively complete when supported by strong friendship, best-friend, found-group, academic, club, or self-definition outcomes.
- Romance should not automatically complete the classmate's personal arc.

### Memory Album Rules

- The memory album should preserve specific shared moments rather than abstract relationship labels.
- For completed arcs, show the scene or object that proves what changed.
- For unfinished arcs, show the strongest almost-moment without shaming the player.
- For distant classmates, show a recognizable school-life image that reminds the player they existed in the same years.
- For best friend, include a recurring private motif that appears in graduation or post-graduation reflection.
- For romance, include a commitment-specific memory but avoid making the rest of the cast feel irrelevant.
- For paths not taken, use subtle absence, empty places, unread posts, missed event photos, or unsent messages rather than punitive failure text.
- The album should not read like a completion checklist.

### Epilogue Writing Rules

- Endings should answer: who did this person become around the player, and what did the player get to witness?
- Write incomplete endings as emotionally real, not mechanically inferior.
- Preserve beautiful incompleteness: some relationships should remain meaningful because they were not fully resolved.
- Avoid savior framing. Even completed arcs should show classmates making their own choices.
- Let academics, clubs, and locations color relationship epilogues when relevant.
- Graduation should feel like the end of a shared era, not a route-results scoreboard.

### Outcome Examples

- **Very close + complete arc + best friend:** The classmate appears prominently in graduation, the memory album uses the private motif, and the epilogue shows an ongoing bond.
- **Close + complete arc + no special bond:** The classmate's core struggle resolves or matures, but their future is not centered on the player.
- **Comfortable + partial arc:** The album preserves one honest moment and one absence, suggesting an important but unfinished connection.
- **Low stage + key Year 1 seed only:** The classmate remains part of the remembered school texture: a hallway image, a post, a rumor, a club photo, or a place associated with them.
- **Romance + incomplete personal arc:** The romance may remain tender but carry unresolved tension; commitment alone should not auto-complete the character's inner story.
