## Extract for Vision Pass

### Provisional One-Sentence Game Description

- A nostalgic high-school life sim where the player spends three school years balancing academics, relationships, and wellbeing under constant time pressure, with their identity emerging from the opportunities they choose and the ones they lose.

### Provisional Player Fantasy

- To live through a more intentional, emotionally meaningful version of adolescence without being able to have everything.
- To shape a personal high-school life path through everyday presence, tradeoffs, and belonging.
- To look back at graduation and feel that a unique life was truly lived, rather than merely optimized.

### Provisional Emotional Promise

- Nostalgia
- Warmth and attachment
- Melancholy or bittersweet reflection

### Provisional Differentiation Signals

- Time is treated as the main emotional and mechanical currency, down to small visible decisions.
- Relationships grow through presence, regularity, compatibility, and shared spaces rather than mostly through scripted quest chains.
- Identity is shaped primarily by chosen activities, time allocation, and developed relationships; clubs are one expression of that, not the whole identity system.
- FOMO comes from too many worthwhile characters, activities, and time-bound events competing for limited time.
- The ending acts as a life interpretation and memory mirror, not just a score screen or route completion summary.
- Smartphone and social media function as social navigation and planning tools inside the simulation, not just UI dressing.

- How directly the game should position itself against Persona-likes, cozy life sims, and management games.

## Audience Extract

- Primary audience: adults nostalgic for their high-school years and interested in revisiting that emotional and cultural setting.
- Adjacent media taste: viewers who enjoy high-school films and TV with rites of passage, teen drama, school events, and familiar social clichés.
- Comparable game taste markers: The Sims, Stardew Valley, Persona 5.
- Experience level: does not require deep game literacy.
- Appeal mix: relationships, cozy narrative, management, and time planning.
- Session expectation: flexible; a single in-game day should fit in under 30 minutes, while the loop should also support longer flow sessions.
- Audience age emphasis: older players looking back on school life rather than current high-school students.
- Commercial outlook: aspirational wide indie success; realistic niche cult-hit profile.

## Core Fundamentals Working Extract

### Provisional Core Loop

1. Review the day's constraints, opportunities, and known schedules through the calendar, school context, and smartphone.
2. Choose how to spend limited time blocks across school, study, social contact, travel, rest, and optional activities.
3. Move through classes and locations, making small but meaningful activity decisions that consume time and affect energy, stress, mood, academics, and relationships.
4. Build or miss relationship opportunities through presence, timing, compatibility, and repeated contact in the right places.
5. End the day or week by absorbing outcomes: stat changes, unlocked events, changed bonds, missed opportunities, and upcoming pressure points.
6. Repeat across weeks and semesters until long-term life-path consequences are reflected in exam outcomes, memories, relationships, and epilogue.

### Provisional Gameplay Pillars

- Time and wellbeing as core currencies: every meaningful action has a visible opportunity cost, while energy, stress, and mood strongly shape what the player can do and how well it works.
- Relationships through engagement: bonds grow through attention, interaction, repeated investment, and care for specific characters.
- Life-path identity through tradeoffs: the player's style emerges from what they prioritize and what they give up.
- School structure as emotional pressure: days, weeks, semesters, exams, and events create pacing and stakes.

### Provisional Primary Decision Forms

- Time allocation appears to be the dominant decision layer.
- Place and availability seem to be the second layer, because where the player goes determines which opportunities and characters are reachable.
- Relationship investment appears to be the third layer, expressed through repeated contact, attention, and compatible activities.
- Wellbeing management is a full resource pillar across the loop, especially through energy, stress, and mood.

### Provisional Tension Hierarchy

1. Limited time and mutually exclusive opportunities.
2. Conflict between academics, relationships, and fun.
3. Calendar pressure from classes, exams, club cadence, and time-bound events.
4. Wellbeing pressure through energy, stress, and mood, which affects execution quality and sustainability.

### Open Questions for User Validation

- Resolved: classes should be full gameplay with their own systems and a substantial share of playtime.
- Resolved: wellbeing should be a full pillar.
- Resolved: the fourth pillar remains school pressure.
- Open for later GDD detail: exact lesson systems, classroom decision model, and how energy, stress, and mood affect each activity category.

## References Extract

### The Sims

- Take: everyday life simulation, arranging a life, and needs as a readable life-management layer.
- Leave: goal-less sandbox structure, building, broad comedy, systemic chaos, open dollhouse play, control of multiple household characters, and simplified relationship modeling.
- Differentiation: `High School Story` offers life simulation inside a focused school setting, with stronger structure, clearer identity formation, and deeper relationship arcs.

### Stardew Valley

- Take: cozy daily rhythm, NPC community, calendar events, activity progression, and the "one more day" loop.
- Leave: farming, crafting, grind economy, and combat.
- Differentiation: `High School Story` shifts the daily loop from production and farming toward school identity, time pressure, wellbeing, and classmates with richer personal stories, problems, and drama.

### Persona 5

- Take: school setting, high-school atmosphere, calendar, systemic time passage, relationship benefits, and the balance between studying and personal life.
- Leave: dungeons, combat, supernatural plot, heavy inventory management, and a strongly linear main story.
- Differentiation: `High School Story` is for players who enjoy Persona's school-life layer but want the school loop itself to be the center of the game.

### Deferred References

- `Growing Up` and `I Was a Teenage Exocolonist` are not currently user-known enough to act as primary references. They can be revisited later as research comparables, not as foundational inspirations.

### Positioning Line

- Stardew-like one-more-day loop, but about school identity, relationships, and time pressure instead of farming, where time, wellbeing, and relationships decide who you became.

## Scope and MVP Extract

- Target platforms for first release: PC-based platforms, primarily Steam.
- Steam Deck: supported if achievable with low additional cost.
- Out of first-release scope: mobile, console, web.
- Later-port possibility: mobile only if the project succeeds and the port becomes justified.
- Engine: Godot.
- Team assumption: solo developer.
- Technical note for later architecture work: custom organized architecture will be needed, but detailed architecture belongs outside the brief.

### MVP Scope

- Duration: one semester.
- Must prove:
  - Time-block daily loop.
  - Lessons as gameplay.
  - Initial relationship interactions, limited to early encounters and relationship onboarding.
  - Wellbeing loop, especially energy and stress.
  - Event calendar.
  - A few first off-school locations.
  - Basic off-school activities, such as cafe and park.
- Explicitly out of MVP:
  - Full relationship arcs.
  - Ending or life-path summary.
  - Finalization of a full game run.

### Scope Risk

- One-semester MVP is still a large solo-dev slice. It should be treated as a restrained vertical slice with limited content depth, not a miniature version of the full game.

## Content and Direction Extract

- Setting: fictional European town.
- Cultural fantasy: school-life experience built from American high-school movie cliches rather than strict local realism.
- Tone: mix of realism and serialized teen drama.
- Player character: custom protagonist.
- Character creation includes:
  - Gender.
  - Initial attributes.
  - Favorites.
  - Preferences such as music, films, and food.
- Attribute effects:
  - Partial aptitude differences for specific school subjects.
  - Compatibility effects with NPCs.
- Romance: important part of the game and one of the most interesting possible run elements.
- Art direction: 2D pixel art.
- Audio direction: lo-fi, cozy, school ambient, with a little teen-drama flavor.

### Classmate Scope

- User is considering 10-15 full classmates for the final game, each with a full classmate story arc.
- Concern: 10-15 classmates may feel small for a school class, but 20+ classmates is likely too large for story arc scope.
- MVP target: 5 classmates, including one with a partial story arc.
- Recommended working direction for brief: define 12 core classmates with full arcs as the production target, plus optional background or lightweight students if the school needs to feel more populated.

## Risks and Open Questions Extract

### Key Risks

- Relationship content scope: 12 full classmates can carry the fantasy, but each full arc adds writing, event scripting, balancing, art, QA, and localization burden.
- Lesson system complexity: lessons are full gameplay, so they need enough depth to matter without turning the game into a classroom minigame collection.
- Wellbeing balance: energy, stress, and mood must matter strongly, but should not make the player feel trapped in maintenance chores.
- Time-pressure tone: the game needs painful tradeoffs, but not a spreadsheet-optimization feel that fights the cozy/narrative positioning.
- MVP size: one semester is valuable for proving rhythm, but still large for solo development unless content is tightly capped.
- Pixel art production: character sprites, portraits, locations, UI, and event moments can become a hidden bottleneck.
- Relationship-romance boundary: romance is important, but the game still needs friendships, identity, school pressure, and personal growth to stay equally meaningful.
- Content pacing: a three-year game needs enough events and variation to avoid repetition, but content volume must stay feasible.

### Open Questions for Later Design

- How do energy, stress, and mood affect different activity categories?
- How many relationship stages does each classmate arc need?
- Which classmates are romanceable, and under what constraints?
- How much of the school population is mechanically meaningful versus background flavor?
- What is the exact semester calendar structure for MVP?
- What are the minimum pixel art asset sets required to prove the MVP without overproducing?
- What Godot architecture patterns will support content-heavy school simulation cleanly?
