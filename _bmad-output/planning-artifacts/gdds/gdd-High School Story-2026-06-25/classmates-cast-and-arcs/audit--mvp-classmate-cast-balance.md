## Purpose

This audit checks whether the five MVP classmates are balanced in narrative richness, distinct in core wounds, and connected enough to feel like one school cast rather than five isolated routes.

The current issue is not that the cast is weak. The issue is resolution imbalance: Charlotte and Eleanor are currently more developed than Oliver, Amelia, and Leo because they share a concrete past rupture and moral conflict.

## Current Imbalance Diagnosis

### Charlotte and Eleanor are in HD

They already have:

- A shared past.
- A betrayal.
- Different versions of the same event.
- Moral debt.
- Present-day behavioral tension.
- A clear future confrontation.

This makes them feel alive because the player can imagine scenes immediately.

### Oliver, Amelia, and Leo need anchors

They each have a good psychological premise, but still need at least one of these:

- A concrete past incident.
- A relationship with another MVP classmate.
- A visible school-system conflict.
- A moment where their defense mechanism hurt someone or cost them something.

Without that, they risk feeling like "good archetype plus wound" rather than fully dramatized classmates.

## Duplication Risks

### Oliver and Amelia

Both currently carry "my future depends on performance."

**Keep the contrast:**  
Oliver's pressure should be bodily, social, and masculine-coded: pain tolerance, team loyalty, being admired for self-damage.

Amelia's pressure should be procedural, invisible, and adultified: planning, applications, recommendations, caretaking, never becoming a problem.

**Avoid:** making Oliver's story mainly about grades/scholarship bureaucracy, or Amelia's story mainly about public popularity.

### Amelia and Leo

Both are about school-system judgment.

**Keep the contrast:**  
Amelia over-identifies with the system because it is her escape. Leo rejects the system before it can reject him.

**Avoid:** making both secretly hardworking academic strivers. Leo's hidden effort should live in skating, repair, art, work, or practical skill first, not homework.

### Charlotte and Amelia

Both can look like perfect girls.

**Keep the contrast:**  
Charlotte performs status and taste. Amelia performs reliability and competence.

Charlotte asks, "Do I look like I belong?"  
Amelia asks, "Did I do enough to get out?"

### Charlotte and Eleanor

Their link is a strength, but it can swallow the MVP.

**Keep the contrast:**  
Charlotte's story is about accountability. Eleanor's is about whether trust is worth exposure.

Charlotte's active sin: she chose status over loyalty.  
Eleanor's active wound: she chose misunderstanding over vulnerability.

## Proposed Equalization Pass

### Oliver Upgrade: Add a Concrete Team Incident

**Accepted anchor:** The Match He Should Not Have Played.

Oliver played through an injury in a visible game or school sports event. The team won, classmates praised him, and adults used him as an example of grit. That praise locked the damage in place: the school rewarded his self-harm before anyone asked if he was okay.

**Added complication:** A younger player or less popular teammate now copies Oliver's "walk it off" behavior. Oliver sees his own wound becoming culture.

**Why this works:**  
It gives Oliver moral texture without making him cruel. He is not only injured; he has become the poster boy for a harmful standard.

**Distinct from others:**  
This is not Charlotte-style social guilt or Amelia-style academic pressure. It is embodied pressure: pain, masculinity, admiration, and team culture.

### Amelia Upgrade: Add a Specific Academic Opportunity and a Quiet Rivalry

**Accepted anchor:** The Recommendation Slot.

There is one limited teacher recommendation, academic program, university-prep opportunity, or competitive school nomination that Amelia needs badly. She has arranged her semester around it.

**Accepted cast connection:** Leo accidentally threatens it, not because he wants it, but because a teacher sees unexpected potential in him after a practical/creative assignment, detention essay, or repair/help moment. Amelia's resentment is not petty; it is terror. Leo seems to receive attention for one flash of promise while she has been bleeding consistency for years.

**Why this works:**  
It makes Amelia's fear active in the school ecosystem and creates a live relationship with Leo.

**Distinct from Charlotte:**  
Charlotte guards social image. Amelia guards a route out.

### Leo Upgrade: Add a Protective Troublemaker Anchor

**Accepted direction:** Leo's rebellion should include a real virtue: sensitivity to other people's suffering.

**Proposed anchor:** The Fight Everyone Misread.

Leo cannot walk past cruelty. When he sees someone being bullied, humiliated, cornered, or socially crushed, he struggles to stay out of it. The problem is that his justice is impulsive: he shoves, threatens, breaks something, starts a confrontation, or humiliates the bully back.

A past serious incident cemented his troublemaker reputation. Main structure: Leo protected a younger/lower-year student Amelia had been trying to help through proper channels, such as tutoring, mentoring, teacher reporting, or careful documentation. Amelia tried to use the system. The system moved too slowly, too cautiously, or too politely.

Leo saw the actual humiliation happen and acted immediately. The school remembered the fight, property damage, threat, or confrontation, not the reason. He did not fully explain himself because doing so would drag the bullied student back into public attention.

Light Eleanor echo: Leo's sensitivity to cruelty can also make him react to jokes or comments about Eleanor, but this should remain character texture rather than the main source of his reputation.

**Accepted incident action:** Phone smash plus public counter-humiliation.

The bullying involved a humiliating recording or threatened upload. Leo took or destroyed the phone to stop the video from spreading. In the same confrontation, he also turned humiliation back on the bully: exposing, mocking, or publicly cornering them in a way that made the intervention morally messier.

This keeps Leo from becoming a spotless misunderstood protector. His instinct is compassionate, but his method can reproduce the same cruelty he hates when he loses control.

**Accepted reason Leo did not tell the full truth:** Victim protection, lost evidence, and shame.

The bullied student did not want the full story dragged into official attention. Leo respected that, even though it made him look worse. The destroyed/deleted phone evidence also made the situation harder to prove. Finally, Leo knew he had crossed a line with the counter-humiliation, so even the truth did not fully absolve him.

**Accepted Amelia interpretation:** Anger, secret relief, and concern for consequences.

Amelia is angry because Leo ruined or bypassed her careful attempt to help through proper channels. She is also secretly relieved that someone finally acted when the system moved too slowly. At the same time, she believes Leo may have made the victim less safe by creating a public incident and pulling more attention toward them.

This makes their mirror dynamic live: Amelia sees Leo's courage and recklessness; Leo sees Amelia's care and overreliance on systems that can fail vulnerable people.

**Core moral rule:** Leo would rather be punished than watch someone be humiliated.

**Cast connection:** This gives Leo a quiet resonance with Eleanor without copying her. Eleanor would rather be misunderstood than exposed again. Leo would rather be punished than make a hurt person explain their hurt publicly.

**Accepted mirror with Amelia:** Amelia trusts rules because rules are her ladder out. Leo distrusts rules because he has seen them protect order more reliably than people.

**Why this works:**  
It gives Leo a virtue, not just a wound. He is not only avoiding failure; he is a boy whose protective instinct comes out sideways in a school system that records consequences more easily than context.
