## Process Notes

This artifact is being built interactively. Only decisions validated by the designer should be treated as accepted direction.

Relationship access rule:

- Core classmate story arcs must not require membership in a specific club.
- Club membership can add variants, insider context, better preparation, lower Stress, stronger outcomes, extra dialogue, club identity texture, or additional flags.
- The essential classmate beat must remain accessible through non-club routes such as Bond/tier threshold, prior flags, Social Media clues, text threads, library/cafeteria/hallway encounters, classmate invitation, public school context, guest/helper roles, or bounded tasks requested by the classmate.
- Route variants are alternate access paths to one canonical relationship beat. The first valid variant encountered advances the beat state and suppresses duplicate variants. Club membership may add insider context, extra choices, lower cost, stronger outcomes, follow-up acknowledgment, or texture, but it must not create a second independent completion of the same emotional beat.

Design starting point:

- Each MVP classmate starts from a recognizable high-school media archetype.
- Each classmate needs a large personal drama that can support a full story arc later.
- MVP uses 5 classmates total:
  - 1 classmate receives a partial 3-beat arc.
  - 4 classmates receive one introduction beat each.
- Preferred naming direction: typical English / British names.

Current preferred archetype set:

- Queen Bee / fashionable popular girl.
- Jock / athlete.
- Goth girl / dark, private, hard to reach.
- Teacher's pet / top student / teachers' favorite.
- Rebel skater / rule-breaker outsider.

## Accepted Classmate Seeds

### Charlotte "Lottie" Fairchild

**Archetype:** Hyper-polished Queen Bee / perfect daughter.

**Accepted name direction:** Charlotte "Lottie" Fairchild.

**Core drama:** A perfect daughter who chose status over the friend who knew her before the crown.

Charlotte's family has recently climbed into a higher social class through business, money, or another visible form of success. They are not relaxed old prestige; they are people trying to look like they have always belonged. Charlotte is part of that performance: polished appearance, correct friends, refined manners, strong social presentation, and no public embarrassment.

At school, this becomes the Charlotte Fairchild persona: immaculate, fashionable, socially powerful, and difficult to catch off guard. The old-money imitation remains the family engine. The Eleanor rupture is the story-arc engine.

**Core wound:** Charlotte is terrified that if she stops looking effortless, everyone will see she was manufactured, and Eleanor remembers enough to prove it.

**Public identity:** Charlotte Fairchild, flawless popular girl.

**Private identity seed:** Lottie, the warmer and less controlled version of herself that Eleanor knew before Charlotte's current social image hardened.

**Important distinction:** Her story should not be "popular girl learns to be nice." Her deeper arc is about a girl raised as a status symbol learning accountability after choosing a constructed identity over a real friendship.

**Likely first impression:** She looks like school is a stage and she already knows every mark, entrance, and audience reaction.

**Likely player misunderstanding:** The player may read her as shallow, vain, or naturally elite. In reality, much of her polish is trained, defensive, and guilt-reinforced.

**Potential secret wants:** To enjoy things her family would see as too ordinary, cheap, childish, unfashionable, or emotionally honest.

**Accepted social weapon:** Fashion as hierarchy / status language, carried with a layer of charm.

Charlotte does not use fashion only because she likes looking good. She has been taught that taste reveals class, belonging, and whether someone is "really" refined or only trying to pass. At school, she understands clothing, styling, photos, compliments, and silence as social signals.

Her approval can raise someone's status. A tiny comment about a bag, shoes, color choice, or photo can embarrass someone without sounding openly cruel. An invitation into a group photo can matter; being left out can matter just as much. Charm keeps the weapon polished: she can make hierarchy feel like warmth until the player notices the edge.

This should avoid a flat "vain mean girl" reading. Charlotte privately has real taste and may genuinely love fabric, color, outfits, and styling. The damage is that her family and social world taught her to use that gift as armor. Her long-term healing direction is for fashion to become self-expression rather than proof of status.

**Accepted prior relationship with Eleanor:** Childhood friends who drifted into an unequal friendship.

Charlotte and Eleanor knew each other before Charlotte's current social image fully hardened. They may have history from primary school, neighborhood life, early secondary school, or another pre-MVP period when Charlotte was still more recognizably "Lottie."

As Charlotte's family status project intensified and her school popularity grew, the friendship became unequal. Eleanor may have still treated Charlotte as a real friend, while Charlotte increasingly treated Eleanor as a private refuge she was not willing to publicly claim. Eleanor remembers the older, less polished Charlotte; Charlotte fears that memory because it proves her current image is constructed.

This makes the rupture more painful than a simple betrayal. Eleanor did not just lose a secret. She lost the person who used to know her, and Charlotte lost the witness to her own unmanufactured self.

**Accepted Charlotte role in the leak:** Status panic plus public non-defense.

Charlotte did not set out to destroy Eleanor, but when the hidden writing profile became dangerous to Charlotte's own social position, she panicked. Someone in the popular circle may have discovered that Charlotte knew about the profile, or pressured her for context, or implied that Charlotte's closeness to Eleanor made her look strange too. To protect herself, Charlotte distanced herself and said too much.

The worse betrayal came afterward. When the rumor spread and Eleanor was recast as obsessive, creepy, or unstable, Charlotte had enough social power to complicate the story, soften it, or publicly refuse it. She did not. Her silence helped the school decide who Eleanor was.

Charlotte's guilt should therefore be specific: not malicious destruction, but cowardice at the exact moment friendship required cost.

**Accepted reason Charlotte stayed silent:** Fear of being seen as fake, fear of losing the crown she had just won, with a lighter fear of social contamination.

Charlotte stayed silent because defending Eleanor would have exposed how fresh and constructed her own status was. Eleanor was living proof that Charlotte had not always been Charlotte Fairchild, flawless Queen Bee. Eleanor remembered Lottie: less polished, more awkward, more hungry to belong.

At the time of the rupture, Charlotte's popularity may have been newly secured rather than stable. Publicly defending Eleanor would have meant risking the crown just as she had learned how to wear it. It also carried social contamination risk: if Charlotte defended the "obsessive goth girl," classmates might wonder whether Charlotte was strange, fake, desperate, or not really one of them.

This makes Charlotte's silence emotionally legible without excusing it. She chose the identity she was building over the friend who knew the girl underneath it.

**Accepted current school persona:** Hyper-polished Queen Bee.

After the rupture with Eleanor, Charlotte became more polished, not less. The lesson she took was not "be kinder" but "never leave a loose thread again." She tightened her appearance, friendships, speech, public reactions, and school-facing identity until nothing looked accidental.

This version of Charlotte should feel almost immaculate at first glance: controlled outfits, controlled smile, controlled social circle, controlled emotional temperature. She avoids visible cracks, odd attachments, and anything that could let classmates connect her to the vulnerable Lottie Eleanor remembers.

Her polish is therefore partly a guilt response. The more she fears the old story returning, the more flawless she tries to become.

**Accepted current treatment of Eleanor:** Cold avoidance plus hidden guilt signals, with polite cruelty in public when contact is unavoidable.

At the start of play, Charlotte mostly acts as if Eleanor does not exist. She does not need to openly attack her; erasure is cleaner and safer. When they must interact publicly, Charlotte uses formal distance and polished politeness, likely choosing "Eleanor" instead of any intimate old nickname.

Small guilt signals can leak through: stopping a comment before it becomes too cruel, leaving useful information where Eleanor can find it, watching her reaction too quickly, or changing the subject when the old rumor resurfaces. These moments should be deniable. Charlotte is not ready to repair the damage, but the damage is not dead inside her.

**Accepted arc spine:** From control to accountability, and from status to loyalty, with "Lottie" returning as the emotional payoff.

Charlotte's arc should not let her solve the Eleanor rupture by managing optics, smoothing language, or privately feeling bad. Her habitual move is control: control the outfit, control the room, control the story, control who is seen with whom. The arc must force her to stop managing the narrative and take responsibility inside it.

The decisive turn is choosing a person over the crown. Charlotte must eventually do something that costs social safety: complicate the rumor, admit her role, defend Eleanor publicly, or refuse the polished version of events that protects Charlotte while leaving Eleanor alone with the damage.

The emotional reward is not that Charlotte stops being stylish or socially powerful. It is that "Lottie" becomes survivable again: the warmer, less manufactured self she buried can return without needing to stay hidden.

**Full-arc breaking point:** Charlotte's constructed effortlessness is exposed at the same time she is forced to face what she did to Eleanor.

The external incident may be a leaked photo, video, thread, or social-media moment that reveals the labor and panic behind her perfect image. The deeper breaking point is moral: Charlotte sees that she did to Eleanor what she fears others will do to her. She let the school turn a private vulnerability into a public label because protecting her crown mattered more than telling the truth.

**Arc question:** What does it cost Charlotte to tell the truth about Eleanor if that truth also exposes who Charlotte used to be?

**MVP suitability note:** Strong candidate for the MVP partial 3-beat arc because she naturally supports social FOMO, Class Integration Day, texting, public/private contrast, the Eleanor tension, and possible romantic chemistry without requiring full romance resolution.

**Light tie with Oliver:** Public symbols of school success.

Charlotte and Oliver can be treated by the school and classmates as the polished faces of success: the fashionable Queen Bee and the golden athlete. They may be placed together in photos, event promotion, school-spirit moments, or casual gossip as an obvious "perfect pair" even if neither asked for that story.

This tie should stay light rather than becoming a second major arc for Charlotte. Its purpose is to show that both characters are rewarded for performing public roles that cost them privately.

**Accepted MVP intro beat:** The Photo Everyone Wants To Be In.

During Class Integration Day, a class photo, school profile board, or integration display needs to be arranged. Charlotte naturally takes control because she "has an eye" for presentation. She appears helpful and elegant, but her choices reveal who gets centered, who is pushed to the edge, and who is quietly erased.

The beat mixes public image management with cutting social grace. Charlotte may give a compliment that sounds refined but marks status, such as calling someone's jacket "brave." Eleanor can pass through the edge of the scene or be mentioned by classmates, for example through a jab like "Wouldn't she curse the camera?" Charlotte's response should be controlled avoidance rather than open cruelty. The player can accept Charlotte's frame, notice exclusion, push for someone marginal to be included, or answer the sharp compliment in their own tone.

### Oliver "Ollie" Grant

**Archetype:** Jock / athlete.

**Accepted name direction:** Oliver "Ollie" Grant.

**Core drama:** The injured golden boy who cannot afford to fall.

On the surface, Oliver is the popular athlete: easy smile, school status, physical confidence, and the sense that things come naturally to him.

Underneath, he is hiding an injury or chronic pain because sport is not only his hobby or social identity. It is his route toward a future: scholarship potential, escape from constraint, family pride, or the one plan that makes his life feel controllable.

**Accepted blend:** Main drama is the injured athlete. Secondary pressure comes from home. A lighter social layer comes from team culture.

Oliver's home life should make the injury more than a sports setback. He may have financial pressure, family instability, a younger sibling he helps care for, or relatives who need his talent to "mean something." If his body fails, he does not only lose status; he loses the future everyone has quietly attached to him.

The team culture reinforces the wound. "Walk it off" is not just a phrase; it is the emotional law he lives under. Oliver may repeat that culture without malice, then later realize he has been asking others to endure the same silence that is hurting him.

**Core wound:** Oliver believes that if his body fails, he fails everyone.

**Arc question:** Can he learn that his value is not the same thing as being unbreakable?

**Likely first impression:** He looks effortless: athletic, popular, approachable, and already surrounded by people.

**Likely player misunderstanding:** The player may read him as carefree or uncomplicated. In truth, his ease is partly camouflage for pain, fear, and responsibility.

**MVP suitability note:** Strong candidate for an intro beat that lets the player notice something others miss: a wince, a hidden brace, a skipped practice detail, or the way he changes the subject when the future comes up.

**Accepted anchor:** The Match He Should Not Have Played.

Oliver played through an injury in a visible game or school sports event. The team won, classmates praised him, and adults used him as an example of grit. That praise locked the damage in place: the school rewarded his self-harm before anyone asked if he was okay.

**Added complication:** A younger player or less popular teammate now copies Oliver's "walk it off" behavior. Oliver can see his own wound becoming culture, but naming that would mean admitting he is not as fine as everyone needs him to be.

**Light tie with Charlotte:** Public symbols of school success.

Oliver and Charlotte are easy for the school to frame together: golden athlete and flawless popular girl, both useful as images of school pride, aspiration, or social desirability. This can create shallow public shipping, event-photo pressure, or assumptions that they understand each other's perfect lives.

The truth is more interesting: both are being praised for masks. Charlotte performs effortless belonging; Oliver performs effortless strength.

**Accepted MVP intro beat:** Walk It Off.

After a school-spirit sports moment, casual practice, or PE-adjacent scene, a younger teammate tries to keep going despite obvious pain because "Ollie would walk it off." Oliver also agrees to help, demonstrate, or stay longer than he should, even though the player can catch a hidden wince or too-careful movement.

The beat should feel warm on the surface and troubling underneath. Oliver is kind, reliable, and admired, but the admiration has turned his self-damage into a rule other students copy. The player can encourage the grit, quietly check on Oliver, support the younger teammate, or simply notice the contradiction.

### Eleanor "Nell" Graves

**Archetype:** Goth girl / dark, private, hard to reach.

**Accepted name direction:** Eleanor "Nell" Graves. At school she is generally Eleanor. "Nell" is reserved for close relationships, old history, or emotionally intimate moments.

**Accepted core direction:** The rumor girl plus the ex-best-friend ghost.

This classmate's goth image is not only aesthetic. It is a social defense built after a friendship rupture and a damaging rumor. The school tells stories about her: that she is cruel, dangerous, manipulative, cursed, or simply "not worth getting close to." She may partly feed the myth because a frightening reputation keeps people at a distance.

The deeper wound is that she used to belong to someone or some group. She may once have been close to a popular classmate, possibly Charlotte or someone in Charlotte's orbit. Something happened: betrayal, public humiliation, a secret exposed, or an incident where she took blame or was misunderstood. Her current image says, "I do not belong to you anymore, and I do not want to."

**Core wound:** If people decide your story for you once, they can do it again.

**Arc question:** Can she let anyone close enough to learn the truth without feeling like she is handing them a weapon?

**Accepted rupture direction:** Hidden heartbreak writing leak plus taking the blame.

Eleanor once kept an anonymous or semi-hidden writing profile where she posted short stories, poems, fragments, and darkly romantic pieces about heartbreak. Much of the work circled the same emotional wound: wanting someone she had lost to come back, replaying what happened, and trying to make art out of a feeling she could not say aloud at school.

At some point, Eleanor trusted Charlotte with the truth that the profile was hers. The secret spread. Once classmates connected the writing to a real ex-boyfriend or classmate, the school flattened her private grief into an uglier label: obsessive, creepy, unstable, unable to move on.

The important truth is that Eleanor's writing should not prove the rumor correct. Her poems and stories may be intense, repetitive, wounded, or embarrassing in the way teenage heartbreak can be, but the cruelty comes from the school reading private emotional art as evidence against her. The rumor turns vulnerability into a weapon.

The deeper truth is not just that she was betrayed. Eleanor may have stayed silent, accepted blame, or allowed the rumor to harden because telling the full truth would expose or destroy someone else: Charlotte, a former best friend, the ex, or the person who first mishandled the secret. Her social exile is partly imposed and partly chosen, because the truth still has a cost.

**Accepted Charlotte connection:** Charlotte was the person Eleanor trusted with the secret. This does not need to mean Charlotte maliciously leaked the profile. Stronger options include: Charlotte told one "safe" person and lost control of the secret; Charlotte protected her own status when the rumor spread; or Charlotte failed to publicly defend Eleanor because doing so would endanger her queen bee image.

**Accepted ex status:** The ex-boyfriend / former romantic focus is not part of the MVP classmate cast.

**Accepted reason for silence:** Eleanor would rather be misunderstood than exposed again.

Eleanor could try to correct the rumor, but doing so would require opening the private wound a second time: showing messages, explaining context, naming what she felt, and letting people inspect the same vulnerability they already mishandled. She may also know that telling the full truth would implicate Charlotte, but the deeper reason is self-protection rather than simple loyalty.

This gives her a clean dramatic rule: when people demand an explanation, Eleanor often chooses the bad version of the story over another public dissection of her private life.

**Likely first impression:** Dark clothes, controlled distance, sharp eyes, and the sense that the room has already judged her before she speaks.

**Likely player misunderstanding:** The player may read her as cold, hostile, or performatively edgy. In truth, some of the edge is a warning label she learned to wear after being socially rewritten.

**MVP suitability note:** Strong candidate for an intro beat built around a rumor, a misread moment, or a chance to believe her over the school's version of her.

**MVP arc role:** Selected for the MVP partial 3-beat arc.

Eleanor is the best MVP proof of relationship progression as trust rather than grind. Her partial arc can move from rumor, to careful contact, to an authorship/consent test during Zine Deadline Crisis without resolving her full story or forcing a complete reconciliation with Charlotte.

**Accepted MVP 3-beat arc skeleton:**

| Beat | Working Name | Relationship Phase | Function |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | The Version Everyone Knows | Stranger Beat | First meaningful contact with the rumor/mask. The player gets a reason to withhold judgment and be curious about Eleanor as a person. |
| 2 | A Piece With No Name | Acquaintance Beat 1 | The player sees the first contradiction: goth rumor girl versus private emotional authorship. Eleanor becomes more specific than her archetype. |
| 3 | Zine Deadline Crisis | Acquaintance Beat 2 / Doorway Into Friendship | First real trust flag around authorship, consent, and boundaries. The beat should make friendship feel reachable without fully resolving Eleanor's secret or the Charlotte rupture. |

Beat 1 should support movement from Stranger toward Acquaintance after the required early interactions. Beat 3 should signal the beginning of friendship, not complete the Friend-phase arc. It should create a story flag such as `nell_trusted_player_with_authorship_boundary` or `nell_saw_player_protect_consent`.

**Accepted club tie:** Literary Club.

Eleanor is the primary MVP Literary Club tie. The club fits her natural domain: private writing, anonymous authorship, poems, fragments, short stories, misreading, consent, and the fear of becoming emotionally visible again.

**Special event tie:** Zine Deadline Crisis.

This event can pressure Eleanor's wound without repeating it cheaply. The key is consent: the drama should test whether writing can become shared safely, not simply expose her again for shock value.

**Arc access guardrail:** Literary Club membership must not be required for Eleanor's MVP 3-beat partial arc.

Her arc should be one core relationship arc with route variants:

- Literary Club member route: the protagonist has insider context and may be better prepared during Zine Deadline Crisis.
- Non-member route: the protagonist can access the same essential beats through Bond/context, text threads, library/cafeteria/hallway encounters, Social Media clues, Nell's invitation, public zine context, or a guest/helper role.
- First valid route wins: if the protagonist triggers a non-club version of a beat before the Literary Club variant appears, that beat is resolved and the club version should not later replay as a separate beat. Club membership can still be acknowledged through follow-up dialogue, added context, or small outcome texture.

The player who joins Literary Club should feel closer to the machinery of the event. The player who does not join should still be able to know Nell, support her, and experience the core MVP arc.

### Amelia Price

**Archetype:** Teacher's pet / top student / teachers' favorite.

**Accepted name direction:** Amelia Price.

**Accepted core direction:** Teacher's favorite because home is chaos, blended with scholarship pressure.

At school, Amelia is composed, prepared, and trusted by teachers. She knows the rules, hands work in early, remembers deadlines, and seems to move through the academic system with clean confidence.

Underneath, school is the only place where effort reliably produces order. Her home life should carry instability, emotional mess, financial pressure, neglect, or parentification. The classroom gives her what home does not: clear expectations, adult approval, and proof that if she performs correctly, something in the world responds.

The scholarship / academic future layer sharpens the drama. Amelia is not ambitious in an abstract way. She needs grades, references, and teacher approval because they are her route out. She cannot afford to be careless, sentimental, or merely "fine." Her future depends on being exceptional.

**Accepted home instability:** Parentification plus invisible neglect.

Amelia helps carry the household in ways most classmates do not see: younger siblings, practical chores, emotional management, forms, reminders, or keeping the day from falling apart. At the same time, her own inner life is easy for others to miss. No one may be openly cruel to her; the quieter wound is that she is noticed most when she is useful, prepared, or impressive.

**Accepted escape route:** University-track obsession.

Amelia is building a long exit path through grades, recommendations, extracurricular credibility, and a spotless academic record. University is not just an aspiration. It is the imagined place where her life finally belongs to her. This makes academic pressure personal: every missed assignment, weak test, or damaged teacher impression feels like a threat to escape.

**Core wound:** Amelia believes that if she stops being useful, excellent, and controlled, the chaos will catch her.

**Arc question:** Can she accept help or desire something for herself without feeling like she is risking her only escape route?

**Likely first impression:** Polished homework, calm answers, teacher trust, and the faint sense that she is already three tasks ahead of everyone else.

**Likely player misunderstanding:** The player may read her as privileged, judgmental, or naturally perfect. In truth, perfection is how she keeps panic organized.

**MVP suitability note:** Strong candidate for an intro beat tied to homework, teacher approval, exam pressure, or an academic opportunity that reveals how much she has riding on school.

**Accepted anchor:** The Recommendation Slot.

There is one limited teacher recommendation, academic program, university-prep opportunity, or competitive school nomination that Amelia badly needs. She has arranged her semester around it because it fits her long university-track escape plan.

**Accepted mirror with Leo:** Leo accidentally threatens this opportunity, not because he wants it, but because a teacher sees unexpected potential in him after a practical/creative assignment, detention essay, repair/help moment, or morally complicated incident. Amelia's resentment is not petty. Leo seems to receive attention for one flash of promise while she has been bleeding consistency for years.

**Accepted club tie:** Science Club.

Amelia is the primary MVP Science Club tie. Science Club is not just a hobby for her; it is resume infrastructure, teacher proximity, proof of initiative, and a route toward the Recommendation Slot.

**Special event tie:** Science Showcase Sprint.

This event should pressure Amelia's need to be exceptional while giving the player ways to help through research, materials/setup, presentation, team support, or fixing a last-minute issue.

**Accepted MVP intro beat:** The Perfect Answer.

During a lesson, teacher check, or academic setup scene, Amelia answers a difficult question almost immediately. The class reads it as ordinary Amelia perfection, but the player can notice that she watches the teacher's reaction less like someone proud and more like someone confirming she is still safe.

The beat then reveals that Amelia has already prepared the relevant form, club signup, deadline note, study plan, or backup copy that everyone else is only now discovering they need. It should feel impressive and faintly funny, but also lonely: Amelia is acting as her own parent, secretary, and emergency plan. The player can admire her competence, accept her help, tease the over-preparation, or notice the exhaustion beneath the polish.

### Leo Ward

**Archetype:** Rebel skater / rule-breaker outsider.

**Accepted name direction:** Leo Ward.

**Accepted core direction:** The boy everyone expects to fail, blended with freedom as avoidance.

Leo looks like the student who has opted out: skating after school, cutting corners, laughing at rules, drifting through lessons, and acting like academic consequences are someone else's religion.

Underneath, the rebellion protects him from a more frightening possibility. If he truly tries and still fails, then the story everyone tells about him might be true. It is safer to perform not caring than to discover whether effort would have been enough.

Skating matters because it is one place where progress feels honest. Falling is normal, repetition is visible, and no teacher's judgment gets to define what the attempt means.

**Core wound:** Leo believes it is better to be dismissed as careless than exposed as not enough.

**Arc question:** Can he risk wanting a future before he knows whether he can reach it?

**Likely first impression:** Hoodie, board, scuffed shoes, late arrival, quick grin, and a practiced talent for making authority look ridiculous.

**Likely player misunderstanding:** The player may read him as lazy, unserious, or naturally defiant. In truth, much of his defiance is pre-emptive self-defense.

**MVP suitability note:** Strong candidate for an intro beat around skipping, detention risk, after-school skating, or a moment where the player sees real effort hidden behind a careless performance.

**Accepted anchor direction:** The Fight Everyone Misread.

Leo cannot walk past cruelty. When he sees someone being bullied, humiliated, cornered, or socially crushed, he struggles to stay out of it. His justice is impulsive, so the school often records the consequence instead of the context.

**Core moral rule:** Leo would rather be punished than watch someone be humiliated.

**Accepted mirror with Amelia:** Amelia trusts rules because rules are her ladder out. Leo distrusts rules because he has seen rules protect order more reliably than people.

**Accepted incident structure:** Leo protected a younger/lower-year student Amelia had been trying to help through proper channels, such as tutoring, mentoring, teacher reporting, or careful documentation. Amelia tried to use the system. The system moved too slowly, too cautiously, or too politely.

The bullying involved a humiliating recording or threatened upload. Leo took or destroyed the phone to stop the video from spreading. In the same confrontation, he also turned humiliation back on the bully: exposing, mocking, or publicly cornering them in a way that made the intervention morally messier.

**Accepted reason Leo did not tell the full truth:** The bullied student did not want the full story dragged into official attention; the destroyed/deleted phone evidence made the situation harder to prove; and Leo knew he had crossed a line with the counter-humiliation, so even the truth did not fully absolve him.

**Accepted Amelia interpretation:** Amelia is angry because Leo ruined or bypassed her careful attempt to help through proper channels. She is also secretly relieved that someone finally acted when the system moved too slowly. At the same time, she believes Leo may have made the victim less safe by creating a public incident and pulling more attention toward them.

**Light Eleanor echo:** Leo's sensitivity to cruelty can make him react to jokes or comments about Eleanor, but this should remain character texture rather than the main source of his reputation.

**Accepted MVP intro beat:** The Phone He Broke.

The player first hears the school version: Leo smashed another student's phone because he is reckless, violent, or looking for trouble. Soon after, the player sees a smaller live echo of the same pattern. Someone makes a cruel joke or starts to socially corner a weaker student, possibly with a phone already out. Leo cuts in with a joke sharp enough to stop the room.

The moment should be funny and uncomfortable at once. Leo protects someone from humiliation, but he does it by humiliating the aggressor back. The player can laugh with him, notice the protected student, challenge the method, or ask about the phone rumor. Leo does not fully explain himself.

## Intro Beat Sanity Guardrails

The MVP intro beats should be framed for the first days or weeks of the first high-school year. The class is new. Some students bring prior-school, local, club, sport, or Social Media reputation fragments, but the whole class should not already understand everyone's full story.

The beats should show reputations forming:

- Charlotte begins becoming the social organizer/Queen Bee rather than already ruling an old class hierarchy.
- Amelia immediately becomes visible as the academic benchmark and teacher-trusted student.
- Oliver's "walk it off" pressure comes through sport/team circles or PE-adjacent context.
- Eleanor's rumor arrives as fragments from prior school, local Social Media, or Charlotte-adjacent networks.
- Leo's phone rumor is partial and incomplete; the live scene suggests there is more to him without fully absolving him.

Each intro beat should imply the wound, not explain the whole backstory. Player choices should express the protagonist's style and priorities rather than offering one obviously correct moral answer.
