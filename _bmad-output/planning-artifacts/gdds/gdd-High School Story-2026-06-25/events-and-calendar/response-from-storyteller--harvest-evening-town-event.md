## Purpose

The MVP town event direction has been narrowed. The selected concept is **Harvest Evening**: an official autumn town harvest market / kiermasz that functions as the seasonal, town-wide counterpart to a fair, without feeling like a generic summer festival.

This handoff is intended for the Game Designer to translate the narrative/event direction into concrete MVP event design: player flow, time-block structure, interaction points, relationship hooks, item usage, Mood/Stress effects, and scope boundaries.

## User Choice

The user selected:

> **Harvest Evening** — a town-organized autumn harvest market / kiermasz.

The user's reasoning:

- It feels like a better autumn equivalent of a town fair.
- It is more official and global than smaller local activities.
- It can plausibly be something the whole town talks about and attends.
- It supports the desired feeling of a community event while still fitting MVP scope.

## Previous Context

The original open slot was:

- School-wide event: `Class Integration Day`.
- Town event: open, now selected as **Harvest Evening**.
- Science Club special event: open.
- Drama Club special event: open.
- Week 20 exam period.

The first working idea, `Autumn Town Fair`, was rejected because it felt too generic and not convincingly autumnal. The revised need was for something closer to an official town event: a calendar anchor that most people in the town recognize, discuss, and may attend.

## Concepts Considered

### 1. Autumn Market Day

An official town market with local food, handmade goods, warm drinks, and small stalls.

**Why it worked:**  
Plausible, town-wide, easy to scope.

**Why it was not selected:**  
It is useful but slightly generic unless given a stronger seasonal ritual or emotional center.

### 2. Founders' Day / Town Anniversary

A town anniversary event with speeches, old photos, local history, and a small public gathering.

**Why it worked:**  
Strong nostalgia and bittersweet reflection.

**Why it was not selected:**  
Less naturally tied to autumn unless the town lore specifically places the anniversary in the semester. It may also lean more civic/historical than warm, social, and teen-facing.

### 3. Harvest Evening

A town-organized autumn harvest market in the park or town square, with food stalls, apple/dynia/pastry themes, warm drinks, local vendors, charity collection, and school volunteers.

**Why it worked:**  
It is explicitly autumnal, town-wide, social, production-friendly, and naturally supports food, gifts, limited time, classmate sightings, and Mood/relationship moments.

**User decision:**  
Selected as the preferred concept.

### 4. Homecoming Street Night

The town supports the school's homecoming with shop displays, snacks, alumni presence, and street activity.

**Why it worked:**  
Strong bridge between school and town.

**Why it was not selected:**  
It risks becoming too school-centered. The town event should contrast with campus life rather than feel like an extension of school programming.

### 5. Autumn Lights Night

The town officially switches on seasonal lights in the park or main street, with a small crowd, food, and music.

**Why it worked:**  
Strong visual identity and emotional atmosphere.

**Why it was not selected:**  
It is evocative, but less grounded in concrete autumn community activity than Harvest Evening.

## Selected Concept: Harvest Evening

### Short Fiction Premise

On a chilly October evening, the town holds its annual **Harvest Evening** in the park. Local vendors, families, and community groups set up modest stalls with apple pastries, pumpkin soup, hot tea, donated produce, handmade trinkets, and seasonal snacks. The school helps with volunteers, but the event belongs to the town, not the campus.

Students attend because everyone knows about it: parents mention it, the convenience store stocks seasonal items, the cafe prepares extra pastries, classmates post location clues, and teachers may casually reference it before the weekend.

### Why It Is Plausibly Autumnal

Harvest Evening uses autumn as the event's reason to exist, not just decoration.

It is grounded in:

- Seasonal food: apples, pumpkin, soup, pastries, cider/tea/cocoa.
- Local harvest and produce themes.
- Earlier dusk and colder weather.
- Community fundraising or food donation.
- Park/town square stalls under temporary lights.
- A semester moment when students are no longer at the beginning, but not yet near exams.

### Gameplay Function

Harvest Evening should act as a fixed calendar event with limited player time.

Possible functions:

- A town-wide social hub for one evening.
- A place to meet classmates outside school roles.
- A source of small purchases, consumables, and gifts.
- A natural use case for food/media/free-time preferences.
- A Social Media clue event: known classmates post where they are or what they are doing.
- A Mood recovery opportunity with possible Stress tradeoffs if crowded, late, expensive, or socially awkward.
- A memory generator based on who the player spends the evening with and what they choose to notice.

### Relationship Opportunities

The event should let different relationship styles emerge through small choices:

- Share food with a classmate.
- Buy a small seasonal gift.
- Help an NPC volunteering at a stall.
- Choose between joining a group or spending time one-on-one.
- Notice an NPC avoiding the crowd at the edge of the park.
- Run into an NPC with family, changing how the player sees them.
- Miss a planned meeting because the player chose another activity.

The strongest emotional texture comes from the fact that the whole town is present, but the player can only meaningfully be with a few people.

### Mood / Memory Potential

Harvest Evening can support both warmth and loneliness.

Positive memories:

- Sharing hot food under string lights.
- Helping a classmate carry supplies.
- Discovering an NPC's favorite seasonal snack.
- Watching the crowd from a quieter spot with someone who dislikes noise.

Bittersweet memories:

- Seeing a classmate with another friend group.
- Arriving too late to meet someone.
- Spending the event alone but noticing the town's warmth from the outside.
- Buying something small and realizing money/time was limited.

### Required MVP Assets or Locations

Use existing scope wherever possible:

- **Park** as the main temporary event space.
- **Cafe** as a supporting location with seasonal food and overflow crowd.
- **Convenience store** as a supporting location with limited seasonal snacks, cheap gifts, or umbrellas/hand warmers if weather is included.
- Phone calendar entry.
- Social Media posts / location clues for known classmates.
- Small item list: hot tea, cocoa, apple pastry, pumpkin soup, candy apple, handmade charm, donation token, seasonal snack.
- Event-specific park dressing: stalls, lights, produce crates, simple crowd ambiance.

### Scope Risk

Overall risk: **Low to medium**.

The concept is production-friendly if constrained to:

- One main temporary park event layout.
- A small number of interactable stalls.
- Text-forward interactions rather than minigames.
- Reused shop systems for purchases.
- Relationship/Mood/memory flags instead of bespoke event mechanics.

Risk increases if the design adds:

- Full festival map.
- Many unique vendors.
- Minigames.
- Complex donation economy.
- Large crowd simulation.
- Unique cutscenes for every NPC.

## Recommended MVP Framing

### Calendar Label

**Harvest Evening**

### Suggested Timing

Mid-semester autumn Friday or Saturday evening, before the exam period becomes dominant.

### Core Player Tension

The town is alive tonight, but the player has limited time, limited money, and limited social bandwidth. They cannot see every stall, buy every item, and spend meaningful time with everyone.

### Emotional Question

When the whole town gathers together, does the protagonist feel like part of it, or like someone passing through?

### One-Sentence Pitch

On a cold October evening, the town gathers for Harvest Evening, and the player must decide who to seek out, what small comfort to buy, and whether the crowd makes them feel more connected or more alone.

## Design Questions for Game Designer

Please define:

- Where Harvest Evening sits in the 20-week semester.
- How many 15-minute activity blocks the event should contain.
- Which locations are accessible during the event.
- Whether attendance consumes the full evening or allows partial attendance.
- Which NPCs should be eligible to appear in MVP.
- How Social Media clues reveal NPC presence without showing full map presence.
- Which purchases are consumables, gifts, or memory triggers.
- How Mood, Stress, Energy, and Money are affected.
- What minimum set of event flags should be created.
- How the event can be missed or poorly used without feeling punitive.

## Example Scene or Moment

The protagonist reaches the soup stall just as the line thins. A classmate is helping behind the table, sleeves rolled up, cheeks red from the cold and steam. They make a joke about smelling like pumpkin for the rest of the semester, then quietly set aside the last warm bowl because they saw the protagonist walking around alone.

The player can:

- Accept and eat together nearby.
- Pay anyway and gain respect.
- Decline and buy something for another NPC.
- Ask why the classmate volunteered.

The scene is small, but it turns a public town event into a personal memory.

## Proposed Memory Flags

- `memory_harvest_shared_food`: Shared a warm seasonal food with an NPC.
- `memory_harvest_helped_stall`: Helped an NPC or townsperson at a stall.
- `memory_harvest_small_gift`: Bought a small seasonal gift for someone.
- `memory_harvest_crowd_edge`: Spent time away from the crowd with an NPC.
- `memory_harvest_missed_meeting`: Missed a planned meeting during the event.
- `memory_harvest_alone`: Attended Harvest Evening alone and watched the town from the outside.

## Handoff Summary

The selected town event is **Harvest Evening**, an official autumn harvest kiermasz organized by the town. It should feel like a seasonal civic event that most residents know about and many attend. Its design value is that it gives the player a believable reason to leave school life behind for one evening while still creating natural social choices with classmates.

The event should remain modest in production, emotionally specific in writing, and grounded in existing MVP systems: time blocks, Mood, Stress, Money, gifts/consumables, preferences, social media clues, relationships, and memory flags.
