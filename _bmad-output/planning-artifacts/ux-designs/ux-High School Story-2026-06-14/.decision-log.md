## Confirmed Session Decisions

### Structural UX

- The phone is the primary game hub and acts as the game's main menu while also
  carrying private surfaces such as messages, profiles, relationships, and the
  memory album.
- The phone opens as a lightweight overlay that occupies roughly one third of
  the screen.
- Opening the phone freezes time and meaningful simulation change, but leaves
  the world visible in the background.
- The phone supports both shallow and deep screens:
  - Calendar: calendar view
  - Map: map view
  - Messages: conversation list and conversation detail
  - Profiles: profile detail view
  - Memory album: photo list and fullscreen single-photo view

### Legibility Model

- Core direction: medium HUD plus progressive legibility.
- Actions and systems begin only partially legible.
- Repeated use reveals more specific effects.
- The UI itself is progressively discovered in front of the player.
- Post-discovery feedback uses a hybrid model with a strong bias toward soft,
  human-readable language instead of spreadsheet labels.
- Time is always numeric and shown in 24-hour clock form.
- Stress and energy should read through icon-and-scale treatment rather than raw
  numeric display.
- Relationship consequences should be phrased as perceived reception by the
  other character, not abstract stat gain.

### HUD And Information Surfaces

- Closed-phone HUD should be relatively rich.
- Accepted default grouping:
  - Always-on: time, location, energy, stress, mood, money
  - Contextual: lateness risk, curfew risk, approaching lesson/event, important
    new message or invitation, academic risk, travel completion
  - Never always-on: relationship detail, full grades, map, calendar, message
    list, profile detail, memory album, detailed action effects

### Dialogue

- Dialogue uses full or near-full spoken lines for choices.
- There are no pre-choice dialogue hints.
- Dialogue rhythm should stay readable and expressive, but not collapse into a
  VN-heavy presentation style.
- Consequences after dialogue should be subtle but readable.
- The trace of a dialogue choice should appear through later character behavior
  and later phone surfaces such as messages, profile tone, and availability,
  not via direct HUD reward.

### Confirmation Model

- Every time-costing action should use a lightweight confirmation.
- Dialogue choices do not.
- Travel and sleep do.
- Confirmation should foreground time cost first, then other important risk.

### Travel And Map

- Travel is chosen from the map, not from a flat list.
- Travel preview should show duration and arrival time.
- Walking between adjacent locations should support stepwise discovery.
- Unknown or undiscovered locations should remain obscured until discovered.

### Overall Presentation

- The game is 2D, tilemap-based, dialogue-heavy, and cutscene-heavy.
- It should read visually as jRPG-adjacent because of the 2D presentation and
  menu density, but it is not genre-defined as a jRPG.
- Classic 2D RPG conversation flow is desired, with a dialogue box, nameplate,
  and high-quality portrait sprites.
- Portrait sprites should appear almost always during dialogue.
- Portrait expression scope should remain pragmatic: a small standard emotion set
  per character rather than a highly granular expression library.

### Lesson UX

- Lessons are the main subsystem that borrows strongly from jRPG combat
  structure.
- Lessons remain 3 turns of 15 minutes each.
- Lesson UI should evoke jRPG clarity and pacing while staying fictionally a
  classroom, not literal combat.
- Accepted persistent lesson action families:
  - Safe actions
  - Risky actions
  - Reactive actions
  - Items
- Teacher attention is semi-readable, not fully metered.
- Some actions can be directed against the teacher or in ways that interact with
  teacher attention, creating punishment risk and reward opportunity.
- Lesson endings should fold back into the day without a battle-results screen.
- Being caught should allow:
  - Immediate turn loss
  - Stress increase or mood loss
  - Academic setback
- Positive play under teacher attention should allow:
  - Academic gain
  - Situation stabilization
  - Mild confidence or mood improvement

## Known Conflicts To Surface

- `docs/game-design.md` and `docs/game-architecture.md` currently describe
  lessons as a shared 3-turn loop with a smaller fixed action vocabulary and
  warn against bespoke subject minigames. The accepted UX direction still keeps
  the 3-turn structure, but adds stronger combat-like framing, items, reactive
  choices, and teacher-attention tension. This should be treated as a proposed
  UX direction pending durable design/architecture sync.
- No explicit visual direction was chosen for color palette, type family, or
  detailed component skinning. `DESIGN.md` will therefore carry clearly marked
  temporary assumptions where the spec requires concrete tokens.
