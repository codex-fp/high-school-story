# Event Cadence And Semester Calendar

## Design Goal

The semester structure should make school life feel alive without overwhelming the player with constant interruption.

For this project, the target cadence is moderate:

- special things happen regularly
- everyday routine still remains the dominant texture of play
- players can anticipate meaningful beats instead of being surprised every day by a new system demand

## Assumptions

- the MVP semester lasts 12 in-game weeks
- school and daily routine remain the backbone of play
- story arcs, academic pressure, and social opportunities should all appear across the semester
- the desired event density is moderate rather than sparse or highly saturated

## Proposed System

The semester should be built from repeating weekly rhythm plus layered event cadence.

Use four event layers:

| Layer | Frequency | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Daily texture | most days | keeps routine alive with light opportunities and consequences |
| Weekly beats | once or a few times per week | creates forward motion and anticipation |
| Arc milestones | every few weeks | gives relationships and story visible progression |
| Semester peaks | a few times per semester | creates memorable anchors and emotional shape |

This avoids two bad outcomes:

- nothing happening for too long
- everything feeling equally important all the time

## Core Player Experience

The player should feel:

- most days are about normal school life and personal planning
- many weeks include at least one meaningful social, academic, or story opportunity
- a few moments per semester feel notably bigger than the rest

## Cadence Target

Because you chose moderate event density, the game should follow this principle:

- routine is the base
- special opportunities are regular accents
- milestone events are spaced enough to feel earned

In practical terms, the player should often think `something is coming up soon`, but not `the game is constantly interrupting me`.

## Event Types

### Daily texture events

These are low-intensity, low-scope moments.

Examples:

- short hallway encounter
- message or invitation prompt
- teacher comment after class
- small mood or social opportunity in dorm or district

Purpose:

- make routine feel reactive
- surface character presence without demanding a full scene
- seed future choices

### Weekly beats

These are medium-importance opportunities that shape the feel of a week.

Examples:

- one classmate asks to meet after school
- a subject becomes more pressing
- a weekend hangout becomes available
- a school rumor or mini social situation appears

Purpose:

- give each week identity
- create planning tension beyond raw stat management
- connect systems to calendar time

### Arc milestones

These are stronger story or relationship progress points.

Examples:

- a vulnerable conversation
- a meaningful personal problem
- a turning point in trust or attraction
- a subject-linked confidence scene

Purpose:

- mark visible progression in a character arc
- justify larger emotional payoff
- create memories the player can recount later

### Semester peaks

These are the biggest anchors in the calendar.

Examples:

- semester opening setup beat
- mid-semester pressure point
- exam week
- end-of-semester resolution beat

Purpose:

- give the semester a beginning, middle, and end shape
- create emotional pacing and reset points

## 12-Week Semester Rhythm

Recommended semester rhythm for MVP:

### Weeks 1-2: settling in

- teach routine
- introduce classmates and baseline schedule
- keep story demands light
- let the player form first habits

### Weeks 3-5: pattern formation

- social and academic priorities become clearer
- first meaningful invitations and arc movement appear
- weak subjects or neglected conditions start becoming visible

### Weeks 6-8: rising pressure

- stronger tradeoffs begin to appear
- more substantial story beats unlock
- the player feels they cannot do everything comfortably

### Weeks 9-10: commitment window

- relationship routes, academic rescue, and key invitations become more decisive
- the game should ask the player what really matters this semester

### Weeks 11-12: exam and resolution

- academic pressure peaks
- some social arcs reach a visible checkpoint
- the semester ends with exam outcome and emotional cooldown

## Weekly Rhythm Template

To keep a moderate but readable cadence, each week should loosely support:

- everyday routine on most weekdays
- one to two medium-significance social or story opportunities
- one academic reminder or pressure nudge
- one weekend highlight opportunity when conditions allow

This is enough to make the week feel alive without feeling scripted every day.

## Weekend Event Role

Weekends should carry a higher chance of memorable activity than weekdays.

Weekend events are the best place for:

- longer hangouts
- relationship milestone scenes
- stronger recovery moments
- more spacious story scenes

This helps weekends feel qualitatively different, not just mechanically emptier.

## Invitation And Opportunity Model

The game should not dump every possible event on the player at once.

Recommended rules:

- present only a small number of meaningful opportunities at a time
- let some invitations expire naturally
- allow some events to return later in softer form when appropriate
- use recent behavior, relationship state, and calendar timing to decide what surfaces

This keeps decision-making readable and helps missed content feel like life tradeoff rather than UI clutter.

## Academic Pressure Integration

The semester calendar should support academics as a recurring pillar, not only an end boss.

Recommended pattern:

- early semester: soft academic reminders
- middle weeks: visible subject drift and catch-up signals
- late semester: stronger exam framing and urgency

This syncs well with the academic progression model already defined.

## Social Arc Integration

Character arcs should not fire on a flat timer.

They should depend on:

- relationship state
- recent attention patterns
- schedule opportunity
- semester timing

This ensures cadence feels authored but still systemic.

## MVP Content Guidance

Because MVP has one fully working lightweight arc with 10 steps, distribute it across the semester rather than frontloading it.

Recommended pacing:

- early semester: 2 introductory steps
- middle semester: 4 development steps
- late semester: 3 escalation steps
- end of semester: 1 payoff or checkpoint step

This prevents the MVP arc from feeling either too sparse or too compressed.

## Anti-Overload Rules

To preserve the cozy-leaning tone:

- avoid stacking multiple high-importance events on the same day by default
- do not force the player to respond to every invitation immediately
- let some weeks breathe more than others
- keep most event triggers legible and foreshadowed

## Balancing Levers

- number of surfaced opportunities per week
- frequency of daily texture events
- spacing between arc milestones
- timing of academic warnings
- weekend event availability
- expiration windows for invitations
- cooldowns between major scenes with the same character

## Risks And Edge Cases

- if event density is too low, the semester feels flat and repetitive
- if event density is too high, routine stops mattering and the game feels noisy
- if major events cluster too tightly, players feel punished for normal planning limits
- if invitations expire too fast, the game feels anxious rather than cozy-leaning
- if arc steps are too evenly spaced, content pacing may feel mechanical instead of natural
